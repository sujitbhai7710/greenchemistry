Prevention of Waste/By-Products

A chemical process/reaction must be carried out in such a manner so as to reduce/minimize or completely eliminate the production of wastes. Waste prevention is necessary because of the following reasons:-

  If a process produces waste, it invokes the need for its treatment/disposal, which in turn amounts to additional expenditure.

  Secondly if the waste is toxic or hazardous, the release of waste in to the environment leads to its pollution, which further invokes the need of treatment, causing additional expenses.

So we must try to devise processes/reactions that minimize the production of wastes to the best possible extent. Since majority of the reactions produce the byproducts in addition to the normal desired product, we may say the byproducts of reactions are wastes.

A measure of waste known as E-factor (Environment factor) has been described by R. A. Sheldon who defined it as the ratio of the mass of waste (kg) to the mass of desired products (kg).

Maximum Incorporation of the Reactants into the Final Product/ Atom economy

Synthetic methods should be designed to maximize the incorporation of all materials (Starting materials and reagents) used in the process into the final product.

The concept of atom economy in chemical synthesis : To synthesise a particular chemical product, all the atoms of the reactants used may not be always incorporated in the desired product. The atoms which are not incorporated in the product are involved to generate the by-product and waste product which may be environmentally hazardous. To have a quantitative idea regarding the amount of waste product and by-product generated in a particular reaction, we can consider the idea of % atom utilization defined as follows :

% Atom utilization = Formula weight of the desired product
                   Formula weight of (desired product + waste and by-product) × 100

But, very often it is difficult to identify the waste and by-products. To avoid this, the concept of % atom economy has been introduced. Atom economy also gives the measure of the unwanted product produced in a particular reaction. It is defined as –

% Atom economy = Formula weight of the desired product
                Sum of formula weight of all the reactants used in the reaction × 100

A fully and correctly balanced equation is used for the calculation of atom economy. The higher the number, the reaction is more economical.

Basic differences between Atom economy (AE) and E-factor: From a balanced stoichiometric equation the atom efficiency calculations can be done without performing any experiment.  
➤ While calculating AE, amount of solvent, acid or base used during work-up are not taken into account as they do not appear in the stoichiometric equation. Whereas during consideration of E-Factor, the actual amount of waste produced in the process is considered. It includes all reagents, solvent-loss and all process aids.  
➤ An ideal atom economy (AE) is 100, whereas ideal E-factor is zero. (Amount of water used in the process is generally avoided in the calculation.)  
Simple way to calculate E-Factor = $\frac{\text{Kg of raw materials} - \text{Kg of desired product}}{\text{Kg of the desired product}}$  

As we move from bulk chemicals (e.g., ammonia, sulfuric acid) to fine chemicals (e.g., pharmaceuticals, agro-chemicals), the E-factor increases significantly. This is because, bulk chemicals are made through highly efficient, continuous processes with minimal waste, whereas, fine chemicals often require multi-step synthesis with lower yields, leading to more waste.  
Also, Fine chemical synthesis involves extensive use of organic solvents, catalysts, and reagents that generate significant waste, but bulk chemical production typically operates in aqueous or gas-phase systems with higher atom economy.  
Typical E-Factor Ranges:  
* Bulk chemicals: <1 (near-zero waste)  
* Fine chemicals: 5-50  
* Pharmaceuticals: 25->100 (due to extensive synthetic steps and purification)  

Thus, transitioning from bulk to fine chemicals results in a significant increase in the E-factor due to lower efficiency, multi-step synthesis, and extensive solvent/reagent use.  

Another term 'Effective Mass Yield' was introduced by Hudlicky  
% Effective Mass Yield (EMY) = $\frac{\text{Mass of desired product}}{\text{Total mass of non-benign reactants used in its preparation}} \times 100$  

EMY suffers from the following drawbacks:  
(a) EMY does not include NaCl, AcOH, etc. which are considered as environmentally benign compounds, but they are not completely benign.  
(b) Defining non-benign is difficult and arbitrary.  

Considering all these, it may be considered that Atom Economy and E-Factor are most acceptable terms in Green Chemistry. AE gives a quick idea of conducting an experiment and E-factor gives the final result after doing the experiment.

Green solvents :

Solvent is the major constituent of a reaction mixture and becomes the prime source of waste mass in a synthetic process. Volatile organic solvents are released into the environment by evaporation, since they are used in much higher proportions than the reagents themselves. Most of the organic solvents suffer from their toxicities, hazards, flammability and explosive nature. They cause pollution in troposphere and depletion of ozone layer in the stratosphere.

The ideal “green” solvent should have a high boiling point, low vapour pressure, no toxicity, dissolve numerous organic compounds, cheap and, naturally recyclable. The main alternative to organic solvents are – i) solvent free process ii) water based chemistry iii) supercritical fluids (particularly water and carbon dioxide) iv) ionic liquids v) fluorous biphasic system etc.
Therefore, the different green solvents used are water, scCO₂, ionic liquids, renewable solvent from bio-mass etc.

Supercritical fluids :

The state of a substance at a temperature and pressure above its critical point is called supercritical fluids, where distinct liquid and gas phases do not exist.
Critical temperature of a substance is the temperature at or above which the gas cannot be liquefied by increasing pressure. At the triple point, all three phases (solid, liquid and gas) coexist. However, the liquid-vapour boundary terminates at a point having critical temperature Tc and critical pressure Pc, this point is called critical point. Above this region it is called supercritical fluid. A supercritical fluid exhibits properties in between a gas and a liquid.

Supercritical carbon dioxide as solvent :

Among CO₂, N₂O, NH₃, n-butane supercritical fluids, carbon dioxide is commonly used because of the following properties: (i) non-toxic (ii) non-flammable (iii) cheap (iv) readily compressible at room temperature (v) readily obtained as by-product in many processes including fermentation of bio-mass (vi) readily available under compressed condition and (vii) critical temperature and pressure can be attained comparatively easily.

Utility of using scCO₂: The critical temperature and pressure of CO₂ are 31.1°C and 73 atm respectively. It possesses properties in between a gas and a liquid. It exhibits diffusivity like a gas and acts as a solvent like light petroleum (a mixture of hydrocarbons like pentanes and hexanes). Such dual properties are very much useful for reactions involving gaseous reagents like H₂ for hydrogenation, O₂ for oxidation and syn gas (mixture of CO, H₂, CO₂) for hydroformylation.

To avoid the organic solvent in extraction process, scCO₂ can be employed as a green replacement of organic solvent. The extracted material can be recovered by releasing the pressure of CO₂. Another advantage of scCO₂ is enhanced safety due to fire-suppression capability of CO₂.  
Solubility of different compounds can be controlled by adjusting pressure and temperature, allowing for precise extraction of desired flavours and active ingredients.

Q: How the activation energy is gained by the substrates in a Diels-Alder (DA) reaction in water?
A. The lateral H-bonding by water around the hydrophobic reactant aggregate imparts a force on the aggregate and it gets energized and reaches the transition state.

Q. How can you explain the rate acceleration of Diels-Alder reaction in water compared to organic solvent?
A. The rate acceleration of Diels-Alder reaction in water compared to organic solvents can be explain primarily due to the hydrophobic effect and transition state stabilization. The mostly nonpolar diene and dienophile are poorly soluble in water, causing them to aggregate to minimize contact with water. This increases their local concentration, raising the chances of a successful reaction.

Additionally, in the activated complex the reactive sites lose their hydrophobic character and interact with the polar solvent water. This stabilizes the transition state through hydrogen bonding, lowering the activation energy and enhances rate of the reaction.

Q. What is 'negative activation volume'? Give an example of a reaction with 'negative activation volume'.
A. If the molar volume of the excited complex is smaller than the total molar volumes of the reactants in the ground state, it is considered as 'negative activation volume'.

The volume of activation will be negative if two molecules in the ground state combine to form a single species in the transition state. So, all cycloaddition reactions are with 'negative activation volume'. Also, for endo product, the molecule is more compact and offers greater negative volume of activation than exo product.

Ionic Liquids (IL) :
Commonly ionic compounds are high melting solids and are mostly inorganic compounds. They form ionic crystals having high lattice energy and high melting point. If cations and anions of ionic compounds are designed in such a way that the ions cannot fit themselves nicely in an ionic crystal, the lattice energy will be very low and hence will have a low melting point. This is the basis of formation of ionic liquid.

Ionic liquid is a low melting ionic compound with melting point less than 100 °C. Those ionic compounds exist in the liquid state at room temperature are called RTionic liquid. In recent years with the awareness of green chemistry, ionic liquid has made its position as a green solvent.

An ionic liquid comprises of a (nitrogen containing) organic cation and inorganic anion. Since one part is large and other is small, it creates asymmetry in the compound which makes it a low melting.

A few special features of ionic liquids are: (i) it is a non-molecular solvent and consists of cations and anions only, (ii) Stable at high temperature which makes them better for carrying out reactions at high temperatures, (iii) low flammability, (iv) non-corrosive,
(v) negligible vapour pressure, which is the most important property that helps it act as a sustainable solvent.

Ionic liquid becomes an attractive alternative to volatile organic solvents which are environmentally not benign (friendly) because of their high vapour pressure; volatile organic solvents continuously make the environment unfriendly. Ionic liquids are mostly combinations of delocalized heterocyclic cations and large sized polyatomic anions. As the ionic liquid contains cations and anions, so varying the nature of the ions the properties of ionic liquid can be tuned according to the need. That's why it is also called a 'designer solvent'.

The first room temperature ionic liquid is alkylammonium nitrate (EtNH₃.NO₃). Most of the ionic liquids are composed of organic cation and inorganic anions. The most common cation employed in ionic liquids is imidazolium cation. Other common cations are ammonium, phosphonium, thiazolium etc.
The anion should be non-nucleophilc to avoid attack of anion on the electrophilic cation. The most common non-nucleophilic anions used in ionic liquids are BF₄⁻, PF₆⁻, AlCl₄⁻. Other anions are triflate, tosylate, sulphonate etc.

Fluorous biphasic system :
Fluorous means having high affinity towards fluorocarbon. High fluorinated compounds are also called Fluorous compound. Fluorous liquids have quite unusual properties and these include high density, high stability (mainly due to the stability of the C–F bond), low dissolving ability and extremely low solubility in water and organic solvents.
Perfluoropentane
(Perfluorous solvents)
Fluorous solvents are non-polar and immiscible with organic solvents or water. Some organic solvents although immiscible with fluorous solvents at room temperature but forms a homogeneous mixture at elevated temperature.
The reactions that take place in fluorous solvents show a somewhat different trend in comparison to the other alternative green solvents. Due to the fact that they are extremely non-polar, they are inappropriate to perform most chemical reactions and are used together with conventional organic solvents to give biphasic mixtures. Use of a fluorous solvent and an organic solvent in a reaction is called a fluorous biphasic solvent (FBS).
In such a biphasic mixture, the soluble reagent or catalyst is in the fluorous phase while the starting materials are dissolved in the immiscible solvent phase, which could be an organic solvent, water or non-organic solvent. These two distinct layers are homogenized upon heating, the reactants come into contact with one another and the reaction takes place. After completion of the reaction, when reaction mixture is cooled, the two layers separate again, with the reaction products remaining in the organic phase while the catalyst remains in the fluorous phase. This situation allows an easy separation of the reaction products and catalyst and the catalyst recycling without the use of an organic solvent for extraction. Such system is non-toxic, can be used several times, and allows the easy separation of the catalyst from the reagents and products.

Fluorous biphasic solvents (FBS) are solvent systems consisting of a highly fluorinated phase (fluorous phase) and a conventional organic or aqueous phase. These systems have unique properties that make them useful in various chemical processes, especially in catalysis and organic synthesis. Here are the key advantages and disadvantages of fluorous biphasic solvents:

Advantages:
- Fluorous solvents are immiscible with most organic and aqueous solvents, allowing easy separation of the reaction product from the catalyst.
- Catalysts dissolved in the fluorous phase can be easily recovered and recycled, reducing waste and costs.
- Solvents provide a chemically inert environment that can improve the stability and longevity of catalysts.
- Recycling of fluorous solvents and catalysts minimizes the need for toxic organic solvents, reducing environmental pollution.

Disadvantages:
- Fluorous solvents and fluorous-tagged reagents are expensive, increasing the overall cost of the process.
- Some fluorinated compounds are persistent in the environment and may show ecological risks. Toxic gaseous fluorine or HF is required for their production.

Q. What characteristics of fluorous solvent that make it a biphasic solvent and why?

A. The characteristics of fluorous solvent that make them biphasic are -
- Fluorous solvents consist mainly of fluorinated hydrocarbons (perfluorinated or partially fluorinated compounds). The strong C-F bonds result in low polarizability and low polarity, which makes them chemically inert and poorly miscible with both water and most organic solvents.
- Fluorous solvents are denser than both water and organic solvents, causes them to settle as a separate layer.
- Fluorous solvents dissolve fluorinated compounds well but have poor solubility for non-fluorinated substances, prevent mixing, resulting in stable phase separation and effective biphasic behaviour.

Immobilized solvents :  
An immobilized solvent refers to a solvent that is bound to a solid support or polymer backbone, preventing it from flowing freely while still allowing it to participate in chemical or catalytic processes. This concept is commonly used in fields like catalysis, chromatography, and biochemistry.  
In green chemistry, an immobilized solvent is used to make chemical processes more environmentally friendly by reducing waste, improving efficiency, and avoiding the use of harmful solvents.  
How immobilized solvent helps in Green Chemistry:  
- Reduces solvent waste: Since the solvent is immobilized (held in place), it doesn't need to be replaced often, which reduces waste.  
- Less pollution: Immobilized solvents prevent solvent evaporation and leakage, cutting down on air and water pollution.  
- Reusable: The immobilized solvent can be used repeatedly, reducing the need for fresh solvents and lowering costs.

Hofmann elimination reaction:
Conventional method: In the conventional Hofmann elimination, an amine is first converted into a quaternary ammonium salt by reaction with excess methyl iodide (CH₃I). The quaternary ammonium iodide is then treated with moist silver oxide to form the corresponding hydroxide, which on heating undergoes β-elimination to give the least substituted alkene (Hofmann product). This method uses expensive silver oxide, requires a high temperature and yields are generally poor and therefore generates considerable waste.

Green approach: The reaction is performed using microwave irradiation, solvent-free conditions, or recyclable solid-base catalysts. Microwave-assisted Hofmann elimination is particularly attractive because it provides rapid heating and improved efficiency. Such reactions can readily be accomplished in water-chloroform mixture with better efficiency.

Diels-Alder reaction:

In organic synthesis Diels-Alder reactions are important single-step, ring-forming cyclo-addition reactions. In the conventional Diels-Alder reaction, a diene reacts with a dienophile in the presence of organic solvents such as benzene, toluene, or xylene, often under reflux conditions. The reaction may require prolonged heating and sometimes Lewis acid catalysts to achieve satisfactory yields. The use of volatile organic solvents and high energy consumption makes the process less environmentally friendly.

Green approach: In the green Diels-Alder reaction, the reaction is carried out in water, or using ionic liquids as green solvents. Microwave or ultrasound irradiation may also be employed to accelerate the reaction. Water often enhances the reaction rate through the hydrophobic effect, leading to higher yields and selectivity. These methods reduce solvent waste, energy consumption, and environmental impact.

Ex: The Diels-Alder reaction of vinylboronates can be easily performed using microwave irradiation giving excellent yields of the cycloadducts. Pinacol vinylboronate was the reagent of choice due to its stability towards hydrolysis, operational simplicity and yields of Diels-Alder products. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example of microwave-assisted Diels- Alder reaction of boron-substituted dienophiles. Subsequent in oxidation of the cyclo-adducts with alkaline hydrogen peroxide afforded the alcohols efficiently.
